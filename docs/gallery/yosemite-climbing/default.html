Yosemite Climbing Routes

A guide to the classics of the Valley

Yosemite Valley is the spiritual home of American big wall climbing. Its towering granite walls have shaped the sport since the 1950s, when pioneers like Royal Robbins, Warren Harding, and Yvon Chouinard pushed what was thought possible. Below are some of the most iconic routes that draw climbers from around the world.

The Nose

El Capitan · Southwest Buttress

Perhaps the most famous rock climb in the world. First climbed by Warren Harding, Wayne Merry, and George Whitmore in 1958 over 47 days using siege tactics. The route follows the prow of El Cap up some of the cleanest granite on Earth, passing legendary features like Sickle Ledge, Dolt Tower, El Cap Tower, Camp 4, Camp 5, the Great Roof, and the Changing Corners.

Grade
5.14a or 5.9 C2
Length
~2,900 ft (31 pitches)
Typical Time
3-5 days
First Ascent
1958

The speed record, held by Tommy Caldwell and Alex Honnold, stands at 1:58:07 (2018).

The Regular Northwest Face

Half Dome · Northwest Face

The first Grade VI in North America, climbed by Royal Robbins, Mike Sherrick, and Jerry Gallwas in 1957. The route ascends the unmistakable sheer face of Half Dome, with classic pitches like the Robbins Traverse and the Zig-Zags. A bucket-list objective for aspiring big wall climbers.

Grade
5.12 or 5.9 C1
Length
~2,000 ft (23 pitches)
Typical Time
1-3 days
First Ascent
1957

Freerider

El Capitan · Salathe Wall variation

A free variation of the Salathe Wall, established by the Huber brothers. Made world-famous by Alex Honnold's ropeless ascent on June 3, 2017, documented in the Oscar-winning film Free Solo. Features the notorious Boulder Problem and the Enduro Corner.

Grade
5.13a
Length
~3,000 ft (33 pitches)
Typical Time
2-5 days
First Free Ascent
1998

The Dawn Wall

El Capitan · Southeast Face

Considered the hardest big wall free climb in the world. Tommy Caldwell and Kevin Jorgeson made the first free ascent in January 2015 after years of effort, captivating global audiences. Features multiple pitches of 5.14 climbing on relentlessly thin granite.

Grade
5.14d
Length
~3,000 ft (32 pitches)
Typical Time
2+ weeks (free)
First Free Ascent
2015

Salathe Wall

El Capitan · Southwest Face

Established by Royal Robbins, Chuck Pratt, and Tom Frost in 1961, the Salathe is often considered the finest rock climb in the world. The route features the iconic Hollow Flake, the Ear, El Cap Spire, and the Headwall. First free climbed by Todd Skinner and Paul Piana in 1988.

Grade
5.13b or 5.9 C2
Length
~3,000 ft (35 pitches)
Typical Time
3-5 days
First Ascent
1961

Astroman

Washington Column · East Face

One of the most celebrated free climbs in Yosemite. First freed in 1975 by John Bachar, Ron Kauk, and John Long, it was considered the hardest free climb in the world at the time. Famous for the Harding Slot and Boulder Problem pitches.

Grade
5.11c
Length
~1,100 ft (11 pitches)
Typical Time
1 day
First Free Ascent
1975

Snake Dike

Half Dome · Southwest Face

The easiest technical route up Half Dome and a Yosemite classic. Features unique dike formations of harder rock protruding from the slab. The approach is long (about 6 miles) and the descent down the cables is iconic. Often climbed in a single big day.

Grade
5.7 R
Length
~800 ft (8 pitches)
Typical Time
1 long day
First Ascent
1965

Royal Arches

North Side of the Valley

A long, moderate classic perfect for getting comfortable on big Yosemite granite. Established in 1936, it weaves up natural ramp systems and includes a famous pendulum. A great introduction to Valley climbing for those new to long routes.

Grade
5.7 A0 or 5.10b
Length
~1,400 ft (15 pitches)
Typical Time
1 day
First Ascent
1936

Grading Quick Reference

  • YDS (Yosemite Decimal System): 5.0-5.15 measures free-climbing difficulty. 5.10+ is considered hard; 5.13+ is elite.
  • Aid Grades: A0-A5 (or C0-C5 for clean aid). Higher numbers mean harder placements and longer fall potential.
  • Commitment Grade: I-VII indicates length and seriousness. Grade VI typically means multi-day on a big wall.
  • R/X: Modifiers indicating runout (R) or potential ground falls (X).

Always climb within your ability. Check current conditions, permits, and seasonal closures with the National Park Service before heading out.
