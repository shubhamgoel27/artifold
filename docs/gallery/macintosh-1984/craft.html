Apple Macintosh, Model M0001

Apple Computer, Inc., Cupertino, California
Introduced January 24, 1984
Object number 84.1.001a–c

Curatorial Note

The Macintosh consolidated, in a single moulded enclosure intended for a domestic desk, research into bitmapped display, the graphical user interface, and the hand-held pointing device that had been undertaken across the previous decade, principally at the Xerox Palo Alto Research Center. Its housing was conceived as an object of furniture rather than of office equipment, with a vertical orientation that obscured the cathode-ray tube and presented the screen at the approximate scale of a printed page. The object enters the collection as a complete unit: central processing enclosure, detachable keyboard, and single-button mouse. It is exhibited in a stable resting state, powered off, with the screen aperture facing the viewer.

Provenance

Industrial design by Jerry Manock and Terry Oyama. System software developed by Andy Hertzfeld, Bill Atkinson, and Bud Tribble. System bitmaps and the original screen typefaces — including Chicago, Geneva, Monaco, and New York — designed by Susan Kare. Project leadership by Steven P. Jobs, succeeding Jef Raskin, who originated the Macintosh project at Apple Computer, Inc. in 1979 and conceived its name. Launch marketing included the one-minute television advertisement titled 1984, directed by Ridley Scott and broadcast during Super Bowl XVIII on January 22, 1984.

Conservation Note

The cathode-ray tube remains an analog component and is subject to gradual loss of cathode emission; the original deflection circuitry has not been operated in display conditions since accession. The polyvinyl chloride housing, originally specified in the manufacturer’s “Fog” finish, is undergoing progressive oxidative yellowing under ambient ultraviolet exposure, a behaviour common to brominated flame- retardant ABS and PVC enclosures of the period; the object is stored at 18°C and 40 percent relative humidity, with ultraviolet-filtered illumination during exhibition. The accompanying 3.5-inch floppy disks are subject to magnetic decay and binder hydrolysis of the oxide layer; the disks have been imaged to archival storage and are no longer mounted for read access. Restoration to operating condition is not anticipated.
