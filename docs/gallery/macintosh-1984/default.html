The Original 1984 Apple Macintosh

The computer that put a graphical user interface in the hands of everyday users.

Introduction

On January 24, 1984, Apple Computer introduced the Macintosh, a compact all-in-one personal computer that became one of the most influential products in the history of consumer technology. Unveiled by Steve Jobs at Apple's annual shareholders meeting in Cupertino, California, the Macintosh was the first mass-market personal computer to feature a graphical user interface and a mouse, fundamentally changing how people interacted with computers.

Priced at $2,495 at launch, the Mac was marketed as "the computer for the rest of us" — a deliberate rejection of the command-line interfaces that dominated the IBM PC era.

Key People Behind the Macintosh

Jef Raskin

Started the Macintosh project at Apple in 1979. He envisioned an inexpensive, easy-to-use appliance computer and named the project after his favorite variety of apple, the McIntosh.

Steve Jobs

Took over leadership of the Mac project in 1981 after being pushed out of the Lisa team. Jobs drove the product's design, marketing, and uncompromising attention to detail, famously insisting on elegance in both hardware and software.

Burrell Smith

The hardware wizard whose elegant digital design squeezed remarkable performance from minimal components. Smith designed the Mac's main logic board.

Andy Hertzfeld

A core member of the original Macintosh software team. Hertzfeld wrote much of the Macintosh's system software, including key parts of the user interface toolbox.

Susan Kare

Designed the Macintosh's original icons, fonts, and visual elements — including the Happy Mac, the Command key symbol, and bitmap fonts like Chicago and Geneva. Her work defined the visual personality of the platform.

Jerry Manock

Led the industrial design of the Macintosh, giving it the distinctive vertical all-in-one enclosure that housed the CRT, motherboard, and floppy drive in a single unit.

The "1984" Commercial

Two days before the Macintosh launch, Apple aired its now-legendary "1984" television commercial during Super Bowl XVIII on January 22, 1984. Directed by Ridley Scott, the 60-second ad depicted a dystopian world inspired by George Orwell's novel 1984, with a young woman hurling a sledgehammer at a giant screen displaying a Big Brother figure — a clear allusion to IBM's dominance of the personal computer market.

"On January 24th, Apple Computer will introduce Macintosh. And you'll see why 1984 won't be like '1984.'"

Cultural Impact

The Macintosh popularized the graphical user interface (GUI), the mouse, and the desktop metaphor for the average consumer. While Xerox PARC had pioneered many of these concepts in the 1970s with the Alto, it was the Mac that brought them into homes, schools, and small businesses.

The Macintosh became the cornerstone of the desktop publishing revolution, especially after the introduction of the Apple LaserWriter (1985) and Aldus PageMaker. For the first time, individuals and small organizations could design professional-quality documents from a desktop computer.

The Mac also influenced design culture broadly — its emphasis on typography, friendly iconography, and human-centered interaction shaped how generations of users would expect software to look and feel. Even today, modern operating systems (including Windows, iOS, and Android) trace their conceptual lineage to ideas the Mac brought to the mainstream.

Legacy

Although the original 128K Macintosh sold modestly compared to the IBM PC and its clones — constrained by its limited RAM and single floppy drive — it laid the foundation for a product line that would, over the next four decades, transform Apple into the world's most valuable company. The Macintosh remains a landmark in computing history: the moment the personal computer became truly personal.
